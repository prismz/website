introduction [#]

tested on kernel version 5.14.21-gentoo

for those who are new to configuring their own linux kernel, the process may seem daunting at first, but there isn't really much to worry about. it's a good idea to have a recovery cd just in case your kernel doesn't boot. i'll do my best to cover each section of the kernel configuration menu.

it's a good idea to read the help section for each option, which will most likely go more in depth than i will. if you have a strange system (one core, weird architecture, etc), it's a good idea to read the help page for options relating to your system's "quirks". much newer and much older systems tend to require extra care when it comes to drivers. if you have a chinese wifi card or a less common piece of hardware you may have to spend more time looking for the right option.

to view your hardware and the corresponding loaded modules, run lspci -k. it may be a good idea to google these options if you're noticing the corresponding hardware not functioning correctly under a custom kernel. for example, my ide controller was not supported until recently by the linux kernel, so i had to enable an option deeper into the menus for my secondary hard drives to be detected.

getting into the menu [#]

go into your kernel source directory (typically /usr/src/linux), and run make menuconfig.
